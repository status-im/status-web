27:T2fcf,<p>A <strong>stablecoin</strong> is a crypto token pegged to a stable asset, usually the US dollar, to reduce price swings. Stablecoins bridge traditional finance and blockchain. They power payments, savings, and collateral without crypto volatility. Three main types exist: collateral-backed, algorithmic, and yield-bearing. Each uses a different method to hold a $1.00 peg.</p><h2 id="what-is-a-stablecoin">What Is a Stablecoin?</h2><p>Stablecoins solve one core problem: price volatility. Bitcoin and Ethereum prices can swing wildly each day. That makes them poor choices for commerce, lending, or savings.</p><p>A stablecoin aims to hold a $1.00 target price. It does this through reserves or supply controls.</p><p>Stablecoins fill four main roles:</p><ul><li>Payment rails inside blockchain networks</li><li>Collateral for lending protocols</li><li>Settlement layers for decentralized exchanges</li><li>Stores of value in volatile markets</li></ul><p>Without stablecoins, DeFi users would need to convert to fiat constantly. Stablecoins remove that friction.</p><h2 id="backed-collateral-backed-stablecoins">Backed (Collateral-Backed) Stablecoins</h2><p><strong>Backed stablecoins</strong> use collateral to hold their peg. That collateral can be fiat currency, crypto assets, or physical commodities.</p><h3 id="fiat-backed-model">Fiat-Backed Model</h3><p>Issuers hold $1 USD in reserve for every token in circulation. This is the simplest and most common model.</p><p>Examples: <strong>USDC</strong> (Circle), <strong>USDT</strong> (Tether).</p><p>How it works:</p><ol><li>A user deposits $1,000 USD with the issuer.</li><li>The issuer mints 1,000 stablecoin tokens.</li><li>The user can redeem tokens back to fiat at a 1:1 rate.</li></ol>

If ETH drops too far, the collateral gets liquidated.</p>

<h2 id="algorithmic-stablecoins">Algorithmic Stablecoins</h2><p><strong>Algorithmic stablecoins</strong> use supply controls instead of collateral. The protocol mints or burns tokens to match demand.</p><h3 id="pure-algorithmic-model">Pure Algorithmic Model</h3><p>If the price rises above $1, the protocol mints new tokens. If the price drops below $1, it burns tokens or offers incentives to reduce supply.</p><p>Example: Terra's <strong>UST</strong> used this model. It failed in May 2022 when user confidence collapsed rapidly.</p>

<h2 id="yield-bearing-stablecoins">Yield-Bearing Stablecoins</h2><p><strong>Yield-bearing stablecoins</strong> hold a $1 peg while earning returns for their holders. Users gain passive income without managing protocols themselves.</p><p>Issuers deposit collateral into external yield sources like lending markets. The protocol sends earned yield back to holders. The peg stays at $1, but the holder's balance grows over time.</p><h3 id="example-sdai">Example: sDAI</h3><p><strong>sDAI</strong> (Savings DAI) is issued by MakerDAO. It deposits DAI into Sky Protocol. Yield flows back to holders on its own, with no manual action required.</p><h3 id="example-gusd">Example: GUSD</h3><p><a href="https://docs.status.network/tokenomics/pre-deposits"><strong>GUSD</strong></a> is a yield-bearing meta-stablecoin on <a href="https://status.network/"><strong>Status Network</strong></a>. It compounds returns from a range of stablecoin strategies.</p><p>It farms yield from USDC, USDT, USDS, and other stablecoins on behalf of the user. It serves as the network's primary savings tool.</p>

<h2 id="regulatory-context-and-market-adoption">Regulatory Context and Market Adoption</h2><p>Stablecoin regulation varies by region. The EU's MiCA framework and US money transmitter laws both shape how stablecoins are designed and issued.</p><p><strong>Fiat-backed stablecoins</strong> like USDC and USDT lead the market by total value. They offer regulatory clarity and in some cases custodial insurance.</p><p><strong>Crypto-collateralized stablecoins</strong> like Dai appeal to DeFi users who prefer decentralized, permissionless systems.</p><p><strong>Yield-bearing stablecoins</strong> attract savers and treasury managers looking for passive returns in DeFi.</p><p>Algorithmic stablecoins remain niche and high-risk after the collapse of Terra's UST.</p><h2 id="stablecoins-on-status-network">Stablecoins on Status Network</h2><p><strong>Status Network</strong> is an Ethereum Layer 2 built for <a href="https://docs.status.network/general-info/gasless-transactions">gasless transactions</a> and capital coordination. Its economic model treats bridged stablecoins as yield-producing collateral.</p><p><strong>GUSD</strong> is built into <strong>Status Network</strong> from the ground up. Users bridge stablecoins to the L2. They then deposit into yield strategies through the <strong>Generic Protocol</strong>.</p><p>That yield funds the <a href="https://docs.status.network/tokenomics/public-funding"><strong>Apps Funding Pool</strong></a>. <a href="https://docs.status.network/tokenomics/karmic-tokenomics"><strong>Karma</strong></a> governance votes decide how the pool distributes capital to builders and projects.</p><h3 id="the-native-yield-engine">The Native Yield Engine</h3><p>The <a href="https://docs.status.network/tokenomics/economic-model"><strong>Native Yield Engine</strong></a> turns deposited stablecoins into productive assets that generate ongoing returns.</p><p><strong>GUSD</strong> is the user-facing product. It earns yield while keeping a $1 peg.</p><p>This reflects the <strong>Status Network</strong> thesis: L2s should run on asset productivity, not on execution fees paid by users.</p><p>Traditional L2s extract revenue from transaction fees. <strong>Status Network</strong> generates revenue from TVL yield and DEX volume instead.</p><p>Users get gasless transactions. Developers get ongoing funding through governance-allocated capital from the yield pool.</p><h2 id="why-stablecoins-matter">Why Stablecoins Matter</h2><p>Stablecoins are the connective tissue of crypto finance:</p><ul><li>Enable decentralized lending and borrowing</li><li>Support cross-chain bridges and atomic swaps</li><li>Allow merchant payments with zero volatility risk</li><li>Provide treasury tools for DAOs and protocols</li><li>Power yield farming and automated investment strategies</li></ul><p>Choosing a stablecoin type depends on what you need:</p><ul><li>Need instant fiat settlement? Use USDC.</li><li>Looking for decentralized borrowing? Use Dai.</li><li>Want to earn yield on your savings? Use sDAI or <strong>GUSD</strong>.</li></ul><p>Yield-bearing stablecoins will likely grow as markets mature. Ecosystems like <strong>Status Network</strong> are built around this idea.</p><p>They structure the L2 so that user collateral generates revenue. That revenue funds public goods and developer incentives directly, with no transaction fees required.</p><h2 id="frequently-asked-questions">Frequently Asked Questions</h2><p><strong>What is the difference between a stablecoin and a central bank digital currency?</strong></p><p>CBDCs are issued by governments. Stablecoins are issued by private entities or protocols on blockchain networks. Stablecoins allow instant global settlement without needing a bank as a go-between.</p><p><strong>Can a stablecoin lose its peg for good?</strong></p><p>Yes. If collateral fails or algorithmic controls break down, the peg can collapse. Fiat-backed stablecoins are the most resistant to this. Pure algorithmic designs carry the highest risk.</p><p><strong>How does sDAI keep a $1 peg while earning yield?</strong></p><p>MakerDAO sends Sky Protocol yield to sDAI holders. The peg stays at $1. The holder's token balance grows as yield builds up over time.</p><p><strong>Are stablecoins regulated like banks?</strong></p><p>Rules vary by country and region. US money transmitter laws apply in some states. EU MiCA requires issuers to meet operational and governance standards. Most fiat-backed stablecoins obtain licenses.</p><p><strong>What happens to stablecoin yield during market downturns?</strong></p><p>Yield depends on the strategy behind the token. If lending rates drop, yield goes down too. In extreme stress, protocols may pause withdrawals or lower rates for a time.</p><p><strong>Can I use a yield-bearing stablecoin like GUSD as collateral in DeFi lending?</strong></p><p>Yes. Yield-bearing stablecoins hold the $1 peg, making them valid collateral. They earn yield while posted, which can lower the effective cost of borrowing.</p><p><strong>What is the advantage of GUSD over holding multiple stablecoins on my own?</strong></p><p><a href="https://hub.status.network/" rel="noreferrer"><strong>GUSD</strong></a> rebalances across diversified yield strategies on its own.
