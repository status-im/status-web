25:T28c1,<p>Gas fees on Ethereum Layer 2 networks are transaction costs paid by users to access block space. They serve three simultaneous functions in every current rollup design:</p><ol><li><strong>Throughput allocation</strong> - determining which transactions get included and in what order</li><li><strong>Spam prevention</strong> - making abusive or speculative transaction volume economically costly</li><li><strong>Revenue generation</strong> - funding sequencer operations, proving costs, and network infrastructure</li></ol><p>These three functions are bundled into a single mechanism: the fee market. As long as fees are high enough, the bundling works. The structural problem is that Ethereum L2 fees are not staying high enough, they are trending toward zero, and the scaling progress driving that trend is irreversible.</p><h2 id="the-data-l2-gas-fees-are-collapsing">The Data: L2 Gas Fees Are Collapsing</h2><p>This is not a forecast. The compression is already documented:</p><ul><li>The <strong>Dencun upgrade</strong> (March 2024) reduced median L2 fees by up to 99% by expanding blob capacity for rollup data availability</li><li>The <strong>Fusaka upgrade</strong> (December 2025) expanded blob capacity further; L2 fees now stabilize around <strong>$0.005–$0.01</strong> per transaction</li><li>Daily Ethereum gas revenue fell from approximately <strong>$23 million at peak</strong> to approximately <strong>$6.3 million in 2025</strong></li><li>On leading OP-Stack rollups including Base, Unichain, and OP Mainnet, MEV search bots consumed <strong>over 50% of all gas</strong> while paying <strong>under 10% of fees</strong>, meaning legitimate users and L2 operators are already subsidizing bot activity at near-zero fee levels</li></ul><p>The direction is structurally determined: as L1 data availability costs fall and L2 competition intensifies, gas fees converge toward zero. Every scaling improvement accelerates this.</p><h2 id="how-fee-compression-breaks-each-gas-function">How Fee Compression Breaks Each Gas Function</h2><h3 id="throughput-allocation-breaks-down">Throughput Allocation Breaks Down</h3><p>When block space costs fractions of a cent, willingness to pay no longer meaningfully distinguishes legitimate users from speculative actors. The fee market's ability to prioritize genuine demand over noise degrades as fees approach zero.</p><h3 id="spam-prevention-fails">Spam Prevention Fails</h3><p>Gas fees were justified in the Ethereum whitepaper as an anti-denial-of-service mechanism, requiring attackers to "pay proportionately for every resource they consume." This logic holds when fees are high enough to make spam economically irrational.</p><p>At near-zero fees, it breaks. On Base, a single successful arbitrage yielding $0.12 profit was preceded by approximately 350 failed probing transactions consuming gas equivalent to four full Ethereum blocks. The aggregate fee cost remained below expected profit. As fees fall further, this ratio only worsens. A sufficiently capitalized attacker can always outspend a gas-based spam mechanism when the cost floor approaches zero.</p><h3 id="revenue-generation-erodes-structurally">Revenue Generation Erodes Structurally</h3><p>Any L2 business model predicated on gas fee extraction faces compression from the same scaling progress that makes the network valuable. TVL grows, user activity grows, protocol usage grows, and gas fee revenue per transaction falls. The economic variables move in opposite directions.</p><h2 id="the-best-current-solution-responsive-pricing">The Best Current Solution: Responsive Pricing</h2><p>At EthCC 2026, Offchain Labs co-founder Edward Felten presented the case for "responsive pricing", a dynamic fee model Arbitrum One adopted in January 2026. <a href="https://cointelegraph.com/news/ethereum-l2s-responsive-pricing-scale-billions">Cointelegraph reported</a> that during peak demand on January 31, Arbitrum's fees remained materially lower than Base and other EIP-1559-based L2s, and included commentary from <a href="https://status.network/">Status Network</a> project lead Cyprien Grau.</p><p>EIP-1559 targets a 50% full block and adjusts the base fee by a maximum of 12.5% per block. Under sudden demand spikes, this slow adjustment produces sharp fee volatility. Responsive pricing aligns fees with real-time network bottlenecks instead, producing flatter fee curves during congestion and lower peak costs.</p><p>This is a genuine improvement. For any L2 operating within a gas-based paradigm, responsive pricing is the correct direction.</p><p>The limitation is that it remains a fee market optimization. As Cyprien Grau, project lead at <a href="https://status.network/">Status Network</a>, <a href="https://cointelegraph.com/news/ethereum-l2s-responsive-pricing-scale-billions">told Cointelegraph</a>: "Responsive pricing makes the decline smoother, but you're still building a revenue model on a depreciating asset."</p><p>Responsive pricing is the most technically sophisticated version of the current model. It does not change what the model is.</p><h2 id="what-replacing-the-gas-model-requires">What Replacing the Gas Model Requires</h2><p>Because gas bundles three distinct functions into one mechanism, replacing it requires purpose-built alternatives for each function independently. Patching the mechanism cannot address a structural trajectory.</p><h3 id="replace-throughput-allocation-with-reputation-based-tiers">Replace Throughput Allocation With Reputation-Based Tiers</h3><p>Transaction quotas should reflect demonstrated contribution to the network rather than willingness to pay in any given block. A reputation-based system, where access is earned through staking, liquidity provision, and sustained participation, aligns block space allocation with value created.</p><p>This also eliminates governance attack vectors inherent to fee markets: a user who has earned throughput through months of genuine contribution has fundamentally different incentives from one who acquired priority in a single block auction.</p><h3 id="replace-spam-prevention-with-cryptographic-rate-limiting">Replace Spam Prevention With Cryptographic Rate Limiting</h3><p>Economic deterrence fails at low fee levels. The replacement is cryptographic enforcement.</p><p><strong>Rate Limiting Nullifier (RLN)</strong> is a zero-knowledge primitive developed by the Ethereum Foundation's Privacy &amp; Scaling Explorations team that enforces per-user transaction rate limits without revealing identity. Compliant usage remains entirely private. Abuse, exceeding the rate limit, triggers secret recovery via Shamir's Secret Sharing, exposing the violator's identity and enabling reputation slashing.</p><p>This is categorically stronger than economic deterrence: a cryptographic rate limit cannot be outspent. RLN has been formally verified by Veridise and independently audited through the yAcademy fellowship program.</p><h3 id="replace-fee-based-revenue-with-yield-backed-network-funding">Replace Fee-Based Revenue With Yield-Backed Network Funding</h3><p>L2 operations should be funded through productive capital rather than transaction tax. ETH bridged to an L2 can be staked on L1 via Lido V3 stVaults, generating yield proportional to TVL. Stablecoins can be deployed into Morpho lending vaults and Sky savings. Native application fees, from DEXs, CDP stablecoins, token launchpads, contribute a second revenue stream proportional to protocol usage.</p><p>Both variables, TVL and protocol usage, grow with the network. Gas fee revenue per transaction shrinks as the network scales. The economic alignment runs in opposite directions.</p><h2 id="the-mass-adoption-benchmark">The Mass Adoption Benchmark</h2><p>Felten framed responsive pricing as necessary to scale Ethereum L2s to billions of users. The goal is correct. The benchmark is not.</p><p>Consumer applications that have achieved billions of users, messaging, payments, social, do not ask users to manage transaction costs. Every gas-related interaction, regardless of how small or predictably priced, is a structural barrier for non-native users: the need to hold native tokens before transacting, gas estimation uncertainty, wallet approval dialogs.</p><p>As Cyp noted to <a href="https://cointelegraph.com/news/ethereum-l2s-responsive-pricing-scale-billions">Cointelegraph</a>: "L2s that scale to billions of users will be the ones where users never think about gas at all, and where networks' economics don't depend on charging them for it."</p><p>Better fee curves do not solve this. Removing gas from the user interaction model does.</p><h2 id="key-definitions">Key Definitions</h2><p><strong>Ethereum L2 gas fees:</strong> Transaction costs on Layer 2 networks paid to access block space. Currently serve throughput allocation, spam prevention, and revenue generation simultaneously, functions that degrade as fees trend toward zero.</p><p><strong>Responsive pricing:</strong> A dynamic L2 fee mechanism that adjusts transaction costs in real time based on actual network demand, replacing EIP-1559's lagging base fee adjustment. Adopted by Arbitrum One in January 2026.</p><p><strong>EIP-1559:</strong> Ethereum's fee market reform (August 2021) introducing a base fee targeting 50% block utilization and a burn mechanism. Standard on most Ethereum L2s.</p><p><strong>Rate Limiting Nullifier (RLN):</strong> A zero-knowledge cryptographic primitive that enforces per-user transaction rate limits without revealing identity. Developed by the Ethereum Foundation's Privacy &amp; Scaling Explorations team. Formally verified by Veridise.</p><p><strong>Gasless L2:</strong> A Layer 2 network where users transact without paying per-transaction gas fees. Execution access is governed by earned reputation rather than fee payment.</p><p><strong>Loss-Versus-Rebalancing (LVR):</strong> The dominant adverse selection cost for AMM liquidity providers, arising from DEX price staleness between blocks. Gasless per-block rebalancing structurally reduces LVR by eliminating the per-transaction cost that makes frequent rebalancing uneconomical.</p><hr><p><em>Status Network is a gasless Ethereum L2 where execution is free for users, spam prevention is cryptographic via RLN, and network economics are backed by productive yield rather than transaction fees.
