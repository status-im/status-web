It funds operations with 30 percent of yield from bridged capital and native ecosystem fees. This creates stable.</p><h2 id="the-four-revenue-channels-for-layer-2-networks">The Four Revenue Channels for Layer 2 Networks</h2><p>Every Layer 2 must fund sequencer operations, data costs, and developer ecosystems. The main models fall into four categories. Each has distinct trade-offs for sustainability, user cost, and decentralization.</p><h3 id="sequencer-fees">Sequencer Fees</h3><p>Most Layer 2 networks run sequencers that order and batch transactions. They post these batches to Ethereum. Users pay per-transaction fees for this service. Costs stay lower than mainnet but remain required for every interaction.</p><p>This model faces structural pressure. As more Layer 2s launch, competition drives fees down. Networks lose revenue and cut developer funding. Ethereum's <a href="https://status.network/blog/what-is-eip-4844-proto-danksharding-l2-fees">EIP-4844</a> reduced data costs by up to 99 percent. That compressed sequencer margins further.</p><h3 id="data-availability-fee-margins">Data Availability Fee Margins</h3><p>Some Layer 2s charge users more than the actual cost of posting data to Ethereum. The gap between user fees and blob space costs creates a margin. Alternative DA layers like Celestia reduce these costs. This squeezes margins across the ecosystem.</p><h3 id="mev-capture-and-redistribution">MEV Capture and Redistribution</h3><p>Sequencers profit from transaction ordering. Some Layer 2s auction priority ordering rights. Others burn MEV revenue to protect users. Some redirect MEV proceeds to protocol treasuries. All approaches depend on sustained volume to generate meaningful revenue.</p><h3 id="native-yield-from-productive-assets">Native Yield from Productive Assets</h3><p>Newer Layer 2s replace sequencer fee dependence with revenue from productive assets on Layer 1. Bridged capital gets deployed into staking and lending protocols. The network captures a commission on generated yield. Users retain most returns.</p><h2 id="how-native-yield-funding-works-in-practice">How Native Yield Funding Works in Practice</h2><p><a href="https://status.network/blog/what-is-gusd-meta-stablecoin">Yield-backed</a> Layer 2s invest bridged assets in established yield protocols. They redirect returns to fund operations, developer programs, and public goods.</p><p>A user bridges 1 ETH. The Layer 2 stakes it via Lido V3 for 3 to 4 percent annual yield. The network captures 30 percent of that yield. Users receive 70 percent.</p><p>Higher total value locked generates more revenue. Users earn yield on their bridged assets. Developers get steady funding. The network sustains without extracting transaction fees.</p><p><strong>Status Network</strong> converts ETH into stETH through <strong>Lido</strong> V3 stVaults. It routes stablecoins into sDAI through <strong>Generic Protocol</strong> via Morpho and Sky. It captures 30 percent of yield for operations and public developer funding pools.</p><h2 id="hybrid-revenue-combining-yield-with-multiple-fee-streams">Hybrid Revenue: Combining Yield with Multiple Fee Streams</h2><p>The most sustainable Layer 2s combine multiple revenue sources. This reduces reliance on any single stream:</p><ul><li>Native yield commission: 30 percent of staking and lending revenue</li><li>DEX swap fees: native exchanges charge 0.1 to 0.3 percent per swap</li><li>Premium transaction pricing: users above free quotas pay optional fees</li><li>Application fees: lending protocols and launchpads contribute fees</li></ul><p><strong>Status Network</strong> uses this hybrid approach. It earns yield from stETH and sDAI plus native <strong>Orvex</strong> DEX swap fees and premium gas fees. These streams fund operations and an apps pool. <strong>Karma</strong> holders vote on how the apps pool gets distributed to builders.</p><h2 id="why-transaction-fees-alone-cannot-sustain-a-layer-2">Why Transaction Fees Alone Cannot Sustain a Layer 2</h2><p>Layer 2 competition drives transaction fees toward zero. Ethereum's blob space expansions reduce data costs further. For Layer 2s charging per transaction, revenue drops when volume falls or fees compress.</p><p>Yield-based models separate revenue from throughput. A network with significant total value locked generating consistent yield earns predictable revenue. Daily transaction count does not affect this income.</p><p>The Dencun upgrade in March 2024 reduced median L2 fees by up to 99 percent. The Fusaka upgrade further expanded blob capacity. Any L2 model built on gas fee extraction faces structural erosion as scaling continues.</p><h2 id="gasless-execution-replacing-the-fee-market-with-reputation">Gasless Execution: Replacing the Fee Market with Reputation</h2><p>Gasless Layer 2s flip the fee model. The network covers execution costs via yield and app fees. Users pay nothing per transaction within their allocated quota.</p><h3 id="rate-limiting-nullifiers-for-spam-prevention">Rate-Limiting Nullifiers for Spam Prevention</h3><p><strong>RLN</strong> (Rate Limiting Nullifiers) uses zero-knowledge proofs to enforce transaction quotas privately. Each user receives a throughput limit based on their reputation tier. They generate a proof showing their quota remains intact. The sequencer processes the transaction at no gas cost.</p><p><strong>RLN</strong> uses <a href="https://status.network/blog/sparse-merkle-tree-smt-rate-limiting">Sparse Merkle Trees</a> (height 20, supporting 1 million accounts). It combines Shamir Secret Sharing with ZKPs. Users exceeding their quota land on a Deny List. They must pay premium fees to continue transacting. Those premium fees flow back to the funding pool.</p><h3 id="karma-soulbound-reputation-governing-access">Karma: Soulbound Reputation Governing Access</h3><p>On <strong>Status Network</strong>, <strong>Karma</strong> is a soulbound token earned through contribution. It cannot be bought, sold, or transferred. Users earn <strong>Karma</strong> through:</p><ul><li>Staking <strong>SNT</strong> tokens (up to 4x multiplier standard, up to 9x with 4-year lock)</li><li>Bridging yield-bearing assets</li><li>Providing <a href="https://status.network/blog/orvex-dex-zero-fee-trading"><strong>Orvex</strong></a> liquidity</li><li>Building or using ecosystem apps</li><li>Paying premium fees when exceeding quotas</li><li>Direct community donations</li></ul><p>More <strong>Karma</strong> unlocks higher free transaction tiers. It also grants stronger governance voting power. <strong>Karma</strong> holders vote on apps pool allocations, DEX gauge weights, and network policy decisions.</p><h3 id="professional-operators-in-a-yield-funded-economy">Professional Operators in a Yield-Funded Economy</h3><p><strong>Status Network</strong> enables professional operators to thrive without competing for MEV. Liquidation bots prevent bad debt on <strong>FIRM</strong> (the <a href="https://status.network/blog/collateralized-debt-position-cdp-firm-usf">CDP stablecoin</a> protocol issuing <strong>USF</strong>). Rebalancing bots maintain optimal ratios across Lido V3 and <strong>Generic Protocol</strong> strategies.</p><p>Market makers on <strong>Orvex</strong> ensure tight liquidity and earn DEX fees. In traditional Layer 2s, bots compete for block space through gas bidding. In a yield-funded model, bots earn from spreads, liquidity provision, and liquidation rewards.</p><h2 id="revenue-model-comparison">Revenue Model Comparison</h2>
