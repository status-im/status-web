20:T2d10,<p>Yield farming is an active DeFi strategy where users move capital between protocols to chase the highest available returns. Native yield is passive. It comes directly from holding productive base assets like staked ETH or lending-optimized stablecoins. The core difference: yield farming requires ongoing effort and carries compounding smart contract risk. Native yield is structural and automatic.</p><h2 id="what-is-yield-farming">What Is Yield Farming?</h2><p><strong>Yield farming</strong> emerged in 2020 as a way for DeFi users to maximize returns on idle capital.</p><p>The basic mechanic: deposit assets into a liquidity pool or lending protocol, receive a reward token, then reinvest that token elsewhere for additional returns.</p><p>Each hop adds yield. Each hop also adds risk.</p><p>Typical yield farming involves:</p><ul><li>Depositing tokens into an <strong>automated market maker (AMM)</strong> like Uniswap or Curve</li><li>Earning swap fees and protocol reward tokens</li><li>Staking those reward tokens in separate contracts</li><li>Repeating across multiple protocols for compounding returns</li></ul><p>Returns can look impressive on paper. In practice, they depend on sustained token emissions, which are inflationary by design.</p><h2 id="the-hidden-costs-of-traditional-yield-farming">The Hidden Costs of Traditional Yield Farming</h2><p>Yield farming rewards are rarely free money.</p><p><strong>Impermanent loss</strong> hits liquidity providers when the relative price of pooled assets shifts. A position that earned 30% APY in fees can still end up underwater if prices diverge enough.</p><p><strong>Token emission risk</strong> is more fundamental. Most yield farming rewards are paid in a protocol's native governance token. If demand for that token drops, so does the yield in real terms.</p><p><strong>Smart contract exposure</strong> multiplies with each protocol added. A position that spans three protocols carries the combined vulnerability of three codebases.</p><p><strong>Gas costs</strong> on Ethereum mainnet can consume a significant share of smaller positions, making frequent rebalancing impractical.</p><p>Sophisticated users can manage these risks. Most retail participants cannot.</p><h2 id="what-is-native-yield">What Is Native Yield?</h2><p><strong>Native yield</strong> is different in structure and intent.</p><p>It originates from economic activity that exists with or without a farming strategy attached. The two cleanest examples:</p><p><strong>ETH staking yield</strong>: When ETH is staked via <a href="https://lido.fi/"><strong>Lido</strong></a> and converted to <strong>stETH</strong>, it earns validator rewards from Ethereum's consensus layer. This yield flows from network security participation, not token emissions.</p><p><strong>Stablecoin lending yield</strong>: When DAI is converted to <strong>sDAI</strong> via <strong>Sky</strong> (formerly MakerDAO), or deployed through <a href="https://morpho.org/"><strong>Morpho</strong></a>, it earns from real borrowing demand. Borrowers pay interest. Lenders receive it.</p><p>Neither of these requires active management. Neither is funded by inflation. The yield is a byproduct of productive capital deployment.</p><h2 id="how-status-network-uses-native-yield-as-infrastructure-funding">How Status Network Uses Native Yield as Infrastructure Funding</h2><p>This is where the model diverges from both traditional L2s and yield farming protocols.</p><p><a href="https://status.network/"><strong>Status Network</strong></a> is a fully gasless Ethereum Layer 2 built on <a href="https://linea.build/">Linea</a>'s zkEVM stack. It has <a href="https://docs.status.network/general-info/gasless-transactions">no gas fee</a> market. Users do not pay to transact.</p><p>The question that model raises: how does the network fund itself?</p><p>The answer is native yield.</p><p>When users bridge ETH to <strong>Status Network</strong>, those assets are deployed into a yield-bearing strategy via <strong>Lido V3 stVault</strong>, producing <strong>stETH</strong>. When users bridge stablecoins, those assets are routed through <strong>Generic Protocol</strong> into <strong>Morpho</strong> and <strong>Sky</strong>, producing yield-bearing equivalents.</p><p>As for March 2026 30% of the yield generated flows to the network's operational costs. The remainder contributes to the <a href="https://docs.status.network/tokenomics/public-funding">apps funding pool</a>, governed by <a href="https://docs.status.network/tokenomics/karmic-tokenomics"><strong>Karma</strong></a> holders.</p><p>This is structurally different from yield farming in a key way: the yield is not a reward for user behavior. It is a property of the capital itself.</p><h2 id="comparing-the-two-models-side-by-side">Comparing the Two Models Side by Side</h2>
