28:T280c,<p><strong>Maximal Extractable Value (MEV)</strong> is profit that block producers or searchers capture by reordering, inserting, or censoring transactions within a block. It acts as a hidden tax on users. Every swap, loan, or trade you submit can be exploited before it settles. MEV costs Ethereum users hundreds of millions of dollars each year through front-running, sandwich attacks, and forced liquidations.</p><h2 id="how-does-mev-work">How Does MEV Work?</h2><p>Blockchains process transactions in blocks. Someone must decide the order. On Ethereum, validators choose which transactions go first. This ordering power creates profit opportunities.</p><p>Specialized bots called <strong>searchers</strong> scan the <strong>mempool</strong>. The <a href="https://www.ledger.com/academy/what-is-a-mempool">mempool</a> is the waiting room for unconfirmed transactions. Searchers look for profitable reordering patterns.</p><p>When a searcher finds one, they pay validators a priority fee. The validator includes the searcher's transaction first. The user's original transaction still executes, but at a worse price.</p><p>This entire process happens in milliseconds. Users rarely notice. They just receive fewer tokens than expected.</p><h2 id="what-are-the-main-types-of-mev">What Are the Main Types of MEV?</h2><h3 id="front-running">Front-Running</h3><p>A bot sees your pending swap. It copies your trade and submits it first with a higher fee. The bot buys before you, pushing the price up. You buy at the inflated price.</p><h3 id="sandwich-attacks">Sandwich Attacks</h3><p>This combines front-running with back-running. The bot buys before your trade and sells right after. Your transaction gets "sandwiched" between two bot transactions. You pay more. The bot pockets the difference.</p><h3 id="liquidation-extraction">Liquidation Extraction</h3><p>In lending protocols, undercollateralized loans can be liquidated. Bots race to trigger these liquidations first. They earn the liquidation bonus. Borrowers lose more collateral than necessary due to timing manipulation.</p><h3 id="back-running">Back-Running</h3><p>A bot places its transaction right after yours. This is common after large trades or oracle updates. The bot captures the arbitrage created by your price impact.</p><h2 id="how-much-does-mev-cost-users">How Much Does MEV Cost Users?</h2><p><strong>MEV</strong> is not a small problem. <a href="https://www.esma.europa.eu/sites/default/files/2025-07/ESMA50-481369926-29744_Maximal_Extractable_Value_Implications_for_crypto_markets.pdf">Regulators</a> using Flashbots data estimate that over 520,000 ETH in realised MEV was extracted on Ethereum between the Merge in September 2022 and mid‑2024, corresponding to hundreds of millions of dollars depending on ETH’s price over that period.</p><p>Most of this value came from DEX traders. Every swap on a decentralized exchange is visible in the mempool. This transparency, meant to ensure trustlessness, becomes a vulnerability.</p><p>The cost appears as <strong>slippage</strong>. You set a 1% slippage tolerance. A sandwich bot takes that full 1%. Your trade still succeeds, but you receive the worst acceptable price instead of the best available one.</p><h2 id="why-does-mev-exist">Why Does MEV Exist?</h2><p>Three conditions create MEV:</p><ul><li><strong>Transparent mempools.</strong> Pending transactions are visible to everyone.</li><li><strong>Flexible ordering.</strong> Block producers choose transaction sequence.</li><li><strong>Financial incentives.</strong> Reordering creates risk-free profit.</li></ul><p>Remove any one condition and MEV shrinks or disappears. This is why different chains take different approaches to the problem.</p><h2 id="what-solutions-exist-for-mev">What Solutions Exist for MEV?</h2><h3 id="private-transaction-pools">Private Transaction Pools</h3><p>Services like <strong>Flashbots Protect</strong> let users submit transactions privately. Searchers cannot see them in the public mempool. This blocks front-running but does not eliminate all MEV.</p><h3 id="mev-aware-dex-designs">MEV-Aware DEX Designs</h3><p>Some DEXs use batch auctions. All trades in a batch execute at the same price. Ordering within the batch does not matter. This removes the profit from front-running individual swaps.</p><h3 id="encrypted-mempools">Encrypted Mempools</h3><p>Transactions are encrypted until the block is finalized. Validators commit to an ordering before seeing transaction contents. This is a promising research direction but adds latency and complexity.</p><h3 id="gasless-architectures-with-rate-limiting">Gasless Architectures with Rate Limiting</h3><p>A fundamentally different approach removes gas-based priority altogether. When users do not compete by paying higher fees, the fee-auction mechanism that enables MEV breaks down.</p><p><strong>Status Network</strong> takes this path. As a <a href="https://status.network/blog/the-dawn-of-nano-transaction-economics-beyond-the-friction-of-gas">gasless Ethereum Layer 2</a> built on the <strong>Linea</strong> zkEVM stack, it eliminates the gas fee market entirely. Transactions are not ordered by who pays the most.</p><p>Instead, <a href="https://status.network/blog/rate-limiting-nullifiers-rln-explained"><strong>Rate Limiting Nullifiers </strong></a><strong>(RLN)</strong> control throughput. RLN uses Sparse Merkle Trees (height 20, supporting 1M accounts), <strong>Shamir's Secret Sharing</strong>, and zero-knowledge proofs. Each user gets a fair allocation of free transactions based on their <strong>Karma</strong> score.</p><p><strong>Karma</strong> is a soulbound, non-transferable reputation token. Users earn it by staking <strong>SNT</strong>, bridging assets, providing liquidity, or building apps. Higher Karma unlocks higher transaction throughput. No one can buy priority. No one can outbid another user for block position.</p><p>This design attacks MEV at its root. Without a fee auction, searcher bots lose their primary weapon: paying validators more to reorder blocks.</p><h2 id="how-does-mev-affect-layer-2-networks">How Does MEV Affect Layer 2 Networks?</h2><p>Layer 2 rollups inherit some MEV risks from Ethereum. The sequencer (the entity that orders L2 transactions) holds the same power as an L1 validator. A centralized sequencer can extract MEV or allow searchers to do so.</p><p>Most L2s today run centralized sequencers. Users trust the operator not to exploit ordering. This is a trust assumption, not a guarantee.</p><p>Decentralized sequencing and alternative ordering rules reduce this risk. Encrypted transaction submission and fair ordering protocols are active areas of research across the rollup ecosystem.</p><p>The <strong>Status Network</strong> model sidesteps the problem differently. Native yield from productive capital (ETH routed through <strong>Lido</strong> V3 stVault, stablecoins through strategies via <strong>Morpho</strong>) funds network operations. The network does not need to extract value from transaction ordering. Revenue comes from yield, not from users.</p><h2 id="can-mev-ever-be-fully-eliminated">Can MEV Ever Be Fully Eliminated?</h2><p>Complete MEV elimination is an open research question. Some forms of MEV, like cross-domain arbitrage, may persist as long as price differences exist across venues.</p><p>The realistic goal is MEV minimization. Reduce the attack surface. Limit what extractors can see. Remove the fee-based priority mechanism. Align incentives so block producers gain nothing from reordering.</p><p>Every layer of protection helps. Private mempools reduce front-running. Batch auctions reduce sandwich attacks. Gasless execution with fair ordering reduces the entire category of fee-priority MEV.</p><p>Users should understand MEV exists. Choose tools and networks that minimize exposure. Check slippage settings. Use MEV-protection services when trading on Ethereum mainnet.</p><h2 id="frequently-asked-questions">Frequently Asked Questions</h2><p><strong>What does MEV stand for in crypto?</strong></p><p>MEV stands for Maximal Extractable Value. It refers to profit that block producers or searchers earn by reordering, inserting, or excluding transactions within a block.</p><p><strong>How do sandwich attacks work in DeFi?</strong></p><p>A sandwich attack places a buy order before your swap and a sell order after it. The attacker profits from the price movement your trade creates. You receive fewer tokens than the open market price offered.</p><p><strong>Does MEV only affect Ethereum mainnet?</strong></p><p>No. MEV exists on any blockchain where transaction ordering creates profit. This includes Layer 2 rollups with centralized sequencers, EVM-compatible chains, and even non-EVM networks with visible mempools.</p><p><strong>How much money is lost to MEV each year?</strong></p><p>Estimates vary, but researchers have documented hundreds of millions of dollars in MEV extraction on Ethereum alone. The true cost is likely higher because not all extraction is easily measured.</p><p><strong>Can a gasless blockchain reduce MEV?</strong></p><p>Yes. When there is no gas fee auction, bots cannot outbid users for transaction priority. <strong>Status Network</strong> removes fee-based ordering entirely, using <strong>RLN</strong> for spam protection instead of gas fees.</p><p><strong>What is the mempool and why does it matter for MEV?</strong></p><p>The mempool holds unconfirmed transactions waiting for block inclusion. Because the mempool is public on most chains, searcher bots can see pending trades and exploit them before they settle.</p><p><strong>Does Karma on Status Network prevent MEV?</strong></p><p><strong>Karma</strong> determines free transaction throughput, not ordering priority. Because Karma is soulbound and non-transferable, no one can buy their way to faster execution. This removes the economic incentive behind most MEV strategies.</p><p><strong>What is the difference between front-running and back-running?</strong></p><p>Front-running places a transaction before yours to profit from your price impact.
