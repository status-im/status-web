27:T2d11,<p>Ethereum rollups are Layer 2 networks that batch thousands of transactions off-chain, then post a compressed proof or record to Ethereum for settlement. <strong>Optimistic rollups</strong> assume transactions are valid unless challenged during a 7-day dispute window. <strong>Zero-knowledge rollups</strong> generate a cryptographic proof for every batch, enabling near-instant finality. Both inherit Ethereum's security.</p><h2 id="why-ethereum-needs-rollups">Why Ethereum Needs Rollups</h2><p>Ethereum's base layer processes roughly 15 transactions per second. That ceiling creates congestion and high gas fees during peak demand.</p><p>Rollups solve this by moving execution off-chain. Hundreds or thousands of transactions are bundled into a single batch. Only the batch summary, plus a validity record, gets posted to Ethereum Layer 1.</p><p>The result: lower cost per transaction, higher throughput, and Ethereum-grade security for every user on the rollup.</p><h2 id="how-rollups-work-the-core-mechanic">How Rollups Work: The Core Mechanic</h2><p>Every rollup follows the same three steps:</p><ol><li>Users submit transactions to the rollup's sequencer.</li><li>The sequencer batches those transactions and executes them off-chain.</li><li>The batch is compressed and posted to Ethereum, along with a security mechanism.</li></ol><p>The security mechanism is where optimistic and zero-knowledge rollups diverge.</p><h2 id="optimistic-rollups-trust-first-verify-if-challenged">Optimistic Rollups: Trust First, Verify If Challenged</h2><p><strong>Optimistic rollups</strong> publish transaction batches to Ethereum without immediate proof. The design assumes all batches are valid by default.</p><p>A challenge window, typically 7 days, allows any third party to submit a fraud proof. If fraud is proven, the batch is rejected and the sequencer is penalized.</p><p>Popular optimistic rollups include <a href="https://arbitrum.io/"><strong>Arbitrum</strong></a> and <a href="https://www.optimism.io/"><strong>Optimism</strong></a>, which built the <a href="https://docs.optimism.io/op-stack/protocol/getting-started"><strong>OP Stack</strong>.</a></p><h3 id="pros-of-optimistic-rollups">Pros of Optimistic Rollups</h3><ul><li>Simpler to implement, especially for EVM compatibility.</li><li>Lower computational overhead per batch.</li><li>Easier migration path for existing Solidity contracts.</li></ul><h3 id="cons-of-optimistic-rollups">Cons of Optimistic Rollups</h3><ul><li>Withdrawals to Ethereum take 7 days due to the challenge window.</li><li>Security depends on at least one honest actor monitoring the chain.</li><li>Fraud proof systems add complexity in practice.</li></ul><h2 id="zero-knowledge-rollups-prove-it-instantly">Zero-Knowledge Rollups: Prove It Instantly</h2><p><strong>Zero-knowledge rollups</strong> (ZK rollups) generate a cryptographic validity proof for every batch before it is posted to Ethereum. This proof is called a <strong>ZK-SNARK</strong> or <strong>ZK-STARK</strong>.</p><p>Ethereum's smart contract verifies the proof. If the proof is valid, the batch is accepted. No challenge period is required.</p><h3 id="pros-of-zk-rollups">Pros of ZK Rollups</h3><ul><li>Near-instant finality once the proof is verified on L1.</li><li>Withdrawals settle in minutes, not days.</li><li>Security is mathematical, not dependent on watchful third parties.</li><li>Stronger privacy properties possible through the ZK proof system.</li></ul><h3 id="cons-of-zk-rollups">Cons of ZK Rollups</h3><ul><li>Proof generation is computationally intensive.</li><li>Historically harder to achieve full <strong>EVM equivalence</strong>.</li><li>More complex engineering overhead.</li></ul><h2 id="how-bots-and-mev-differ-across-rollup-types">How Bots and MEV Differ Across Rollup Types</h2><p>Rollup architecture shapes the economics of on-chain bots. Liquidation bots, arbitrage searchers, and per-block rebalancers all behave differently depending on finality speed and fee structure.</p><p>On optimistic rollups, the 7-day settlement window creates uncertainty for MEV extraction. Searchers must factor in challenge risk when calculating expected value.</p><p>On ZK rollups, fast finality reduces that uncertainty. Arbitrage bots can close positions faster, and liquidation bots can act with higher confidence that state is final.</p><p><strong>Loss-versus-rebalancing (LVR)</strong> is also relevant. LVR measures the cost that liquidity providers bear when arbitrage bots trade against stale AMM prices. Faster finality on ZK rollups narrows the staleness window, reducing LVR for LPs.</p><p>Gasless networks like <a href="https://status.network/"><strong>Status Network</strong></a> add another dimension. When users pay no gas, bot economics shift. Spam protection via <a href="https://docs.status.network/general-info/gasless-transactions"><strong>RLN</strong></a> (Rate Limiting Nullifiers) replaces gas as the access control layer, preventing bot-driven congestion without pricing out real users.</p><h2 id="what-is-a-zkevm">What Is a zkEVM?</h2><p>A <strong>zkEVM</strong> is a zero-knowledge rollup that is fully compatible with the Ethereum Virtual Machine. Developers deploy standard Solidity contracts without modification.</p><p>Early ZK rollups required custom toolchains. <strong>zkEVM</strong> networks closed that gap, combining ZK proof security with EVM programmability.</p><p>This is now the frontier of Ethereum scaling. Projects like <a href="https://linea.build/"><strong>Linea</strong></a>, built by Consensys, operate as production-grade zkEVM networks.</p><h2 id="side-by-side-comparison">Side-by-Side Comparison</h2>
