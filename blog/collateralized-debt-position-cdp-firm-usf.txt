20:T278b,<p>A <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Collateralized_debt_obligation"><strong>collateralized debt position</strong></a> (CDP) lets you lock crypto assets in a smart contract and mint stablecoins against them. You keep exposure to your collateral while borrowing a stable asset. If your collateral value drops too far, the protocol liquidates it to protect the system. <a href="https://status.app/blog/introducing-firm-the-native-stablecoin-of-status-network"><strong>FIRM</strong></a> is a CDP protocol on <strong>Status Network</strong> that issues the <strong>USF</strong> stablecoin using this model.</p><h2 id="how-does-a-collateralized-debt-position-work">How Does a Collateralized Debt Position Work?</h2><p>A CDP has three core steps: deposit, mint, repay.</p><p>You deposit crypto (like ETH) into a smart contract. The contract locks your deposit as collateral.</p><p>The protocol lets you mint stablecoins worth less than your deposit. This gap is the "collateral ratio."</p><p>A 150% ratio means you lock $150 of ETH to mint $100 of stablecoins. The extra $50 acts as a safety buffer.</p><p>To get your collateral back, you repay the minted stablecoins. The contract unlocks your deposit.</p><h3 id="what-happens-if-collateral-drops-in-value">What Happens if Collateral Drops in Value?</h3><p>Every CDP has a liquidation threshold. If your collateral falls below it, the protocol sells enough to cover the debt.</p><p>This protects stablecoin holders. Their tokens stay backed even during price crashes.</p><p>Most CDP protocols charge a stability fee. This is an interest rate on your open position. It keeps the system funded.</p><h2 id="why-do-cdps-matter-in-defi">Why Do CDPs Matter in DeFi?</h2><p>CDPs solve a core problem. Crypto holders want liquidity without selling their assets.</p><p>Selling ETH to get dollars means losing future upside. A CDP lets you borrow against ETH instead.</p><p>You stay long on your collateral. You get a stablecoin to use elsewhere.</p><h3 id="cdps-vs-lending-protocols">CDPs vs Lending Protocols</h3><p>Lending protocols like <strong>Aave</strong> or <strong>Compound</strong> match borrowers with lenders. Depositors supply assets. Borrowers pay interest to those depositors.</p><p>CDPs work differently. No lender exists. The protocol itself mints new stablecoins against your collateral.</p><p>This means CDP stablecoins are "native" to the protocol. <strong>MakerDAO</strong> pioneered this model with DAI.</p>

<h2 id="what-is-firm-protocol">What Is FIRM Protocol?</h2><p><strong>FIRM</strong> is a CDP stablecoin protocol on <strong>Status Network</strong>, an Ethereum Layer 2. It issues <strong>USF</strong>, a stablecoin backed by crypto collateral locked in FIRM vaults.</p><p>FIRM follows the proven CDP model. Users deposit collateral, mint <strong>USF</strong>, and repay later to reclaim their assets.</p><h3 id="what-makes-firm-different">What Makes FIRM Different?</h3><p>FIRM operates on a gasless Layer 2. On <strong>Status Network</strong>, users do not pay gas fees for transactions. The network funds execution through native yield, not user fees.</p><p>This changes the economics of opening a CDP.</p><p>On Ethereum mainnet, opening and managing a CDP costs gas. During congestion, gas fees can reach $50 or more per transaction.</p><p>On <strong>Status Network</strong>, those costs disappear. Users interact with <strong>FIRM</strong> without worrying about transaction fees.</p><h3 id="how-usf-fits-the-status-network-economy">How USF Fits the Status Network Economy</h3><p><strong>USF</strong> is one piece of a larger system.</p><p><strong>Status Network</strong> generates revenue from native yield. Lower ratios mean more borrowing power but higher liquidation risk.</p><p>Consider two users, each depositing $10,000 of ETH.</p><ul><li>User A mints $5,000 of stablecoins (200% ratio). ETH must drop 50% before liquidation.</li><li>User B mints $6,666 of stablecoins (150% ratio). ETH must drop only 33% before liquidation.</li></ul><p>User B gets more liquidity. User A gets more safety.</p><p>Choosing a collateral ratio depends on your risk tolerance and market outlook.</p><h2 id="what-is-a-cdp-stablecoin">What Is a CDP Stablecoin?</h2><p>A CDP stablecoin is any token minted through collateralized debt positions. Its value stays near $1 because:</p><ul><li>Every token is backed by locked collateral worth more than $1</li><li>Liquidation mechanisms remove undercollateralized positions</li><li>Arbitrage incentives keep the price anchored</li></ul><p>DAI from <strong>MakerDAO</strong> is the most well known CDP stablecoin. <strong>USF</strong> from <strong>FIRM</strong> follows the same category.</p><p>CDP stablecoins differ from centralized stablecoins like USDC. No bank holds dollars in reserve. Instead, smart contracts enforce collateral rules onchain.</p><h2 id="the-role-of-governance-in-cdp-systems">The Role of Governance in CDP Systems</h2><p>CDP protocols need governance to set key parameters:</p><ul><li>Accepted collateral types</li><li>Minimum collateral ratios</li><li>Stability fees (interest rates)</li><li>Liquidation penalties</li></ul><p>On <strong>Status Network</strong>, <strong>Karma</strong> holders govern network parameters. <strong>Karma</strong> is a soulbound reputation token earned through contributions. It cannot be bought, sold, or transferred.</p><p>This reputation-based governance model means parameter decisions reflect actual participation, not purchased voting power.</p><h2 id="when-should-you-use-a-cdp">When Should You Use a CDP?</h2><p>CDPs work best when you want to:</p><ul><li>Hold a crypto asset long term while accessing liquidity now</li><li>Avoid selling and triggering a taxable event</li><li>Leverage your position (mint stablecoins, buy more collateral)</li><li>Participate in DeFi with a decentralized stablecoin</li></ul><p>CDPs carry risk. If prices drop fast, liquidation can happen before you react.
