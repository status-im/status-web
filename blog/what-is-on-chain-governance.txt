20:T2c98,<p>On-chain governance lets participants vote on protocol decisions without intermediaries. Results live on-chain. Smart contracts run them right away. Two models lead today: token voting, where asset ownership sets power, and reputation voting, where earned soulbound credentials set power. <a href="https://status.network/"><strong>Status Network</strong></a> uses the second model through its <a href="https://hub.status.network/karma" rel="noreferrer"><strong>Karma</strong></a> system.</p><h2 id="what-is-on-chain-governance">What Is On-Chain Governance?</h2><p>On-chain governance is a decision system. Participants vote using cryptographic proof stored on a blockchain.</p><p>No single executive calls the shots. Power flows to token holders, liquidity providers, or reputation earners.</p><p>A typical flow begins with a proposal. Others debate it off-chain. Voting opens on-chain for a set window.</p><p>Smart contracts run the approved result on their own. No central authority needed.</p><p><a href="https://ethereum.org/"><strong>Ethereum</strong></a> DAOs, <a href="https://aave.com/"><strong>Aave</strong></a>, and <a href="https://makerdao.com/"><strong>MakerDAO</strong></a> all use this model. Votes stay permanent and open to audit.</p><h2 id="how-does-token-voting-work">How Does Token Voting Work?</h2><p>Token voting ties power to asset ownership. One token equals one vote. Bigger holders hold more sway.</p><p><strong>Pros:</strong></p><ul><li>Simple to build</li><li>Liquid and easy to move</li><li>Works with staking contracts and delegation</li></ul><p><strong>Cons:</strong></p><ul><li>Plutocratic: wealth turns into voting weight</li><li>Whales can buy tokens just to steer results</li><li>Short-term traders can flip key votes</li><li>Vote-buying stays hard to spot</li></ul><p><strong>Compound</strong>, <strong>Uniswap</strong>, and <strong>Aave</strong> all use token voting.</p><p>A holder of 1 million COMP tokens has 10,000x more power than someone with 100 tokens.</p><p>This opens the door to governance capture. Coordinated actors buy enough tokens to push decisions toward their own gain.</p><h2 id="how-does-reputation-based-governance-work">How Does Reputation-Based Governance Work?</h2><p>Reputation systems swap transferable tokens for credentials that cannot move between wallets. These are soulbound tokens (SBTs) like Karma by Status Network.</p><p>SBTs reflect real contributions. They cannot be bought, sold, or traded.</p><p>Voting power comes from actions: staking, adding liquidity, building apps, or using the network.</p><p><strong>Pros:</strong></p><ul><li>Resists plutocracy: wealth alone does not buy votes</li><li>Resists sybil attacks: earning reputation takes repeated genuine work</li><li>Lines up rewards with long-term health</li><li>Blocks vote-buying by design</li></ul><p><strong>Cons:</strong></p><ul><li>Harder to design fairly</li><li>Cold-start challenge: building early reputation takes time</li><li>Gaming risk: some may try to exploit earning rules</li><li>Slower path to full voting power</li></ul><h2 id="how-does-status-networks-karma-system-work">How Does Status Network's Karma System Work?</h2><p><strong>Status Network</strong> runs reputation governance through <strong>Karma</strong>, a soulbound token that cannot move between wallets.</p><p><strong>Karma</strong> is earned. Never bought. Never sent to others.</p><p>Users earn <strong>Karma</strong> by:</p><ul><li><a href="https://hub.status.network/stake">Staking <strong>SNT</strong></a> (up to 9x boost for a 4-year lock)</li><li>Bridging yield-bearing assets like ETH or DAI</li><li>Adding liquidity on <a href="https://www.orvex.fi/"><strong>Orvex</strong></a>, the native DEX</li><li>Building or using apps on the network</li><li>Paying premium gas fees after passing a free quota</li><li>Donating to the public <a href="https://docs.status.network/tokenomics/public-funding">funding pool</a></li></ul><p>Standard transactions run on <a href="https://docs.status.network/tokenomics/economic-model">native yield</a>. No gas top-ups needed.</p><p>New users can onboard privately with no funding trail.</p><p><strong>Karma</strong> holders vote on three areas:</p><ol><li><strong>Apps Funding Pool</strong>: Sending yield and DEX fees to developers.</li><li><strong>Liquidity Rewards</strong>: Setting gauge weights for DEX pools on <strong>Orvex</strong>.</li><li><strong>Network Settings</strong>: Choosing splits between native yield, DEX fees, and premium gas fees.</li></ol><p><strong>Karma</strong> is soulbound. Whale hoarding is blocked by design.</p><p>A founder with $100 million cannot buy governance control. They must contribute and wait.</p><h2 id="bot-economics-and-governance-gaming">Bot Economics and Governance Gaming</h2><p>Reputation systems face a real threat: automated gaming.</p><p>Liquidation bots, arbitrage bots, and rebalancing bots can push high transaction volume. If app-usage metrics feed <strong>Karma</strong> directly, bots could build reputation without genuine work.</p><p><strong>Status Network</strong> counters this with earning source variety. No single path rules. Staking, bridging, building, and donating all count. Gaming one path does not yield outsized reputation.</p><p><a href="https://docs.status.network/general-info/gasless-transactions"><strong>Rate Limiting Nullifiers (RLN)</strong></a> add another guard.</p><p><strong>RLN</strong> uses Sparse Merkle Trees (height 20, holding 1 million accounts). It pairs Shamir's Secret Sharing with zero-knowledge proofs to cap each account's throughput.</p><p>Accounts that pass their quota land on a Deny List. They pay premium gas until the quota resets.</p><p>Bots that spam get throttled and pay more. Real users stay gasless.</p><h2 id="token-voting-vs-reputation-voting-direct-comparison">Token Voting vs Reputation Voting: Direct Comparison</h2>

If 10 whales control 51% of tokens, governance is centralized in practice.</p><p>Reputation systems flip this. Longer contribution means more power. New entrants cannot buy control on day one.</p><p>Sybil attacks grow costly. Each account must perform genuine actions on its own. Running thousands of fake accounts turns irrational at scale.</p><p>The key design challenge: if staking rewards rule the earning model, wealthy users still win by staking more. Many paths cut single-vector capture.</li><li>What does a sybil attack cost? Higher cost means stronger defense.</li><li>Has governance capture happened before? Check past votes for whale coordination.</li></ol><p>Token voting is simpler to build. The earning rate stays steady, making governance capture far harder than in token-voting systems.</p><p><strong>What is vote delegation in on-chain governance?</strong></p><p>Vote delegation lets a token holder assign voting power to another address without moving ownership. If you lack time or knowledge to vote, you delegate to a trusted community member. They vote for you but cannot touch your tokens.</p><p><strong>How does Karma differ from governance tokens like AAVE or COMP?</strong></p><p><a href="https://hub.status.network/karma" rel="noreferrer"><strong>Karma</strong></a> cannot be moved and is earned through contributions. <strong>AAVE</strong> and <strong>COMP</strong> can be moved and bought on open markets. This stops <strong>Karma</strong> from piling up among wealthy buyers who never helped the network.</p><p><strong>Why pick reputation voting if it is harder to build?</strong></p><p>Reputation voting resists governance capture. Wealthy actors cannot control outcomes through capital alone.</p><p><strong>How does Status Network stop bots from gaming Karma?</strong></p><p><a href="https://docs.status.network/general-info/gasless-transactions"><strong>RLN</strong></a> caps each account's throughput using zero-knowledge proofs and a Deny List.
