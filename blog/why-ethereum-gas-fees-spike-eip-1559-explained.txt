1e:T23b5,<p>Ethereum gas fees spike when user demand for block space exceeds supply. <a href="https://ethereum.github.io/abm1559/notebooks/eip1559.html"><strong>EIP-1559</strong></a>, activated in August 2021, replaced blind auctions with a dynamic <strong>base fee</strong> that rises during congestion and falls when blocks are quiet. This made fees more predictable but did not eliminate spikes.</p><p>Layer 2 networks solve the root problem by moving execution off Ethereum mainnet entirely.</p><h2 id="what-are-ethereum-gas-fees">What Are Ethereum Gas Fees?</h2><p>Every Ethereum transaction requires computation. Gas measures that computation. You pay gas fees to compensate validators for processing your transaction.</p><p>Fees are priced in <strong>gwei</strong>, a tiny fraction of ETH. One gwei equals 0.000000001 ETH.</p><p>A simple ETH transfer costs roughly 21,000 gas units. A complex DeFi swap might cost 200,000 or more. Multiply the gas units by the price per unit to get your total fee.</p><h2 id="why-do-gas-fees-spike">Why Do Gas Fees Spike?</h2><p>Ethereum blocks have a limited size. Each block can hold a fixed amount of gas. When more people want transactions than a block can fit, competition for that space drives prices up.</p><h3 id="common-spike-triggers">Common Spike Triggers</h3><ul><li><strong>NFT mints</strong>: A popular drop sends thousands of transactions at once.</li><li><strong>DeFi liquidations</strong>: Market crashes trigger cascading on-chain actions.</li><li><strong>Token launches</strong>: New token sales create sudden, intense demand.</li><li><strong>Airdrop claims</strong>: Millions of wallets rush to claim tokens in the same window.</li><li><strong>Network attacks</strong>: Spam transactions can flood the mempool and crowd out real users.</li></ul><p>During the 2021 NFT boom, gas fees regularly exceeded $100 per transaction. In calmer periods, the same transaction costs under $1.</p><p>The pattern is always the same. Demand surges. Block space stays fixed. Prices explode.</p><h2 id="how-eip-1559-changed-the-fee-market">How EIP-1559 Changed the Fee Market</h2><p>Before <strong>EIP-1559</strong>, Ethereum used a first-price auction. Users bid a gas price. Validators picked the highest bids. This was chaotic and unpredictable.</p><p>EIP-1559 introduced three key changes.</p><h3 id="the-base-fee">The Base Fee</h3><p>Every block now has a <strong>base fee</strong>. This is the minimum price per gas unit for inclusion. The protocol sets it algorithmically.</p><p>If the previous block was more than 50% full, the base fee increases. If less than 50% full, it decreases. The maximum change is 12.5% per block.</p><p>This creates a predictable pricing curve. Users can estimate fees more accurately.</p><h3 id="fee-burning">Fee Burning</h3><p>The base fee is <strong>burned</strong>, meaning destroyed permanently. It does not go to validators. This reduces ETH supply over time.</p><p>Since August 2021, millions of ETH have been burned through this mechanism. During high-congestion periods, more ETH is burned than created, making ETH deflationary.</p><h3 id="priority-tips">Priority Tips</h3><p>Users can add a <strong>priority fee</strong> (tip) on top of the base fee. This tip goes directly to the validator. Higher tips incentivize faster inclusion during busy periods.</p><p>The total fee formula is straightforward:</p><p><strong>Total fee = (base fee + priority tip) x gas units used</strong></p><h2 id="what-eip-1559-does-not-fix">What EIP-1559 Does Not Fix</h2><p>EIP-1559 improved fee predictability. It did not increase block capacity. It did not lower fees during peak demand.</p><p>When every block is full, the base fee still climbs rapidly. The 12.5% adjustment per block compounds quickly. In sustained congestion, fees can double in under a minute.</p><p>The core problem remains: Ethereum mainnet processes roughly 15 to 30 transactions per second. Global demand regularly exceeds that throughput.</p><p>EIP-1559 made the auction fairer. It did not make blocks bigger.</p><h2 id="how-layer-2-networks-solve-the-fee-problem">How Layer 2 Networks Solve the Fee Problem</h2><p><strong>Layer 2</strong> (L2) networks execute transactions off Ethereum mainnet. They inherit Ethereum's security by posting proofs or data back to L1.</p><p>This approach increases total throughput by orders of magnitude. More capacity means less congestion. Less congestion means lower fees.</p><h3 id="types-of-layer-2-solutions">Types of Layer 2 Solutions</h3><ul><li><strong>Optimistic rollups</strong>: Assume transactions are valid. Use fraud proofs if challenged.</li><li><strong>ZK rollups</strong>: Generate cryptographic proofs of validity. No challenge period needed.</li></ul><p>Both models batch hundreds of transactions into a single L1 submission. Users share the L1 posting cost across the entire batch.</p><p>A swap that costs $5 on Ethereum mainnet might cost $0.05 on a rollup. During L1 congestion spikes, the savings become even more dramatic.</p><h2 id="can-gas-fees-reach-zero">Can Gas Fees Reach Zero?</h2><p>Most Layer 2 networks still charge small fees. <a href="https://status.network/"><strong>Status Network</strong></a> takes a different approach. It is a gasless <strong>Ethereum Layer 2</strong> built on the <strong>Linea</strong> zkEVM stack.</p><p>Users on <strong>Status Network</strong> pay no gas fees at all. The network funds execution through its <a href="https://status.network/blog/the-dawn-of-nano-transaction-economics-beyond-the-friction-of-gas"><strong>native yield engine</strong></a>. Bridged assets like ETH and DAI are converted to yield-bearing forms (stETH, sDAI). A portion of that yield covers operational costs.</p><p>Spam prevention uses <strong>Rate Limiting Nullifiers</strong> (<a href="https://docs.status.network/overview/general-info/gasless-transactions/">RLN</a>) instead of fees. Each user receives a free transaction quota based on their <strong>Karma</strong> score, a soulbound reputation token earned through contribution.</p><p>This model eliminates gas as a user-facing cost. No fee estimation. No failed transactions from underbidding. No wallet-draining spikes during congestion.</p><h2 id="monitoring-current-gas-fees">Monitoring Current Gas Fees</h2><p>Several tools help you track Ethereum gas prices in real time:</p><ul><li><strong>Etherscan Gas Tracker</strong>: Shows current base fee and priority fee estimates.</li><li><strong>Blocknative Gas Estimator</strong>: Provides probability-based fee predictions.</li><li><strong>Ultrasound.money</strong>: Tracks ETH burn rate and supply dynamics from EIP-1559.</li></ul><p>For best results, time your L1 transactions during low-demand periods. Weekends and early morning UTC hours tend to have lower base fees.</p><p>Or use a Layer 2 and skip the problem entirely.</p><h2 id="frequently-asked-questions">Frequently Asked Questions</h2><p><strong>Why do Ethereum gas fees spike during NFT mints?</strong></p><p>NFT mints create sudden demand from thousands of wallets competing for limited block space.
