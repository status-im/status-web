The main models fall into four categories. Each has distinct trade-offs for sustainability, user cost, and decentralization.</p><h3 id="sequencer-fees">Sequencer Fees</h3><p>Most Layer 2 networks run sequencers that order and batch transactions. They post these batches to Ethereum. Users pay per-transaction fees for this service. Costs stay lower than mainnet but remain required for every interaction.</p><p>This model faces structural pressure. As more Layer 2s launch, competition drives fees down. Networks lose revenue and cut developer funding. Ethereum's <a href="https://status.network/blog/what-is-eip-4844-proto-danksharding-l2-fees">EIP-4844</a> reduced data costs by up to 99 percent. That compressed sequencer margins further.</p><h3 id="data-availability-fee-margins">Data Availability Fee Margins</h3><p>Some Layer 2s charge users more than the actual cost of posting data to Ethereum. The gap between user fees and blob space costs creates a margin. Alternative DA layers like Celestia reduce these costs. This squeezes margins across the ecosystem.</p><h3 id="mev-capture-and-redistribution">MEV Capture and Redistribution</h3><p>Sequencers profit from transaction ordering. Some Layer 2s auction priority ordering rights. Others burn MEV revenue to protect users. Some redirect MEV proceeds to protocol treasuries. All approaches depend on sustained volume to generate meaningful revenue.</p><h3 id="native-yield-from-productive-assets">Native Yield from Productive Assets</h3><p>Newer Layer 2s replace sequencer fee dependence with revenue from productive assets on Layer 1. Bridged capital gets deployed into staking and lending protocols. The network captures a commission on generated yield. Users retain most returns.</p><h2 id="how-native-yield-funding-works-in-practice">How Native Yield Funding Works in Practice</h2><p><a href="https://status.network/blog/what-is-gusd-meta-stablecoin">Yield-backed</a> Layer 2s invest bridged assets in established yield protocols. They redirect returns to fund operations, developer programs, and public goods.</p><p>A user bridges 1 ETH. The Layer 2 stakes it via Lido V3 for 3 to 4 percent annual yield. The network captures 30 percent of that yield. Users receive 70 percent.</p><p>Higher total value locked generates more revenue. Users earn yield on their bridged assets. Developers get steady funding. The network sustains without extracting transaction fees.</p><p><strong>Status Network</strong> converts ETH into stETH through <strong>Lido</strong> V3 stVaults.
