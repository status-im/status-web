Think of it like postage for a letter. The heavier (more complex) the letter, the more stamps you need.</p><p>Every operation on the <strong>Ethereum Virtual Machine (EVM)</strong> has a fixed gas cost. Sending ETH costs 21,000 gas units. A token swap might cost 150,000 or more.</p><p>You do not pay in "gas" directly. You pay in ETH. The total fee equals gas units multiplied by the price per unit.</p><h2 id="how-are-gas-fees-calculated">How Are Gas Fees Calculated?</h2><p>Since the <strong>EIP-1559</strong> upgrade in August 2021, Ethereum uses a two-part fee structure:</p><ul><li><strong>Base fee</strong>: set by the protocol based on block demand. This portion is burned (destroyed).</li><li><strong>Priority fee (tip)</strong>: an optional amount you add to incentivize validators to include your transaction faster.</li></ul><p>The formula looks like this:</p><p><strong>Total Fee = Gas Units x (Base Fee + Priority Fee)</strong></p><p>If a simple transfer uses 21,000 gas and the base fee is 30 gwei (0.00000003 ETH), the cost is about 0.00063 ETH. At $3,500 per ETH, that is roughly $2.20.</p><p>Complex smart contract calls use far more gas. Minting an NFT or executing a DeFi trade can cost 200,000 to 500,000 gas units.</p><h2 id="why-do-gas-fees-spike">Why Do Gas Fees Spike?</h2><p>Ethereum processes roughly 15 transactions per second. Block space is limited. When more people want to transact than the network can handle, a bidding war begins.</p><p>High-demand events cause fee spikes:</p><ul><li>Popular NFT mints</li><li>Token launches and airdrops</li><li>DeFi liquidation cascades</li><li>Market volatility driving urgent trades</li></ul><p>During peak congestion, base fees can jump 10x to 50x in minutes. A $2 transfer can become a $50 transfer. Users with low-value transactions get priced out entirely.</p><p>This is not a bug. It is the intended design. Gas fees ration scarce block space through price.</p><h2 id="who-gets-the-fees">Who Gets the Fees?</h2><p>Under <strong>EIP-1559</strong>, the base fee is burned. This removes ETH from circulation permanently.</p><p>The priority fee goes to validators who propose and attest to blocks. Validators earn these tips on top of their staking rewards.</p><p>Burning the base fee creates deflationary pressure on ETH supply. When network usage is high enough, more ETH is burned than issued. This makes ETH "ultrasound money" in the community's framing.</p><h2 id="what-is-a-layer-2-and-how-does-it-help">What Is a Layer 2 and How Does It Help?</h2><p>A <strong>Layer 2 (L2)</strong> is a separate execution environment that processes transactions off the Ethereum mainnet. It then posts compressed proofs or data back to Ethereum for final security.</p><p>L2s reduce fees in three ways:</p><ul><li><strong>Batching</strong>: hundreds of transactions are compressed into a single Ethereum transaction, splitting the cost.</li><li><strong>Cheaper execution</strong>: computation happens on the L2, where block space is abundant.</li><li><strong>Optimized data</strong>: rollups post only the minimum data needed for verification.</li></ul><p>The two main types of L2 rollups are:</p>
